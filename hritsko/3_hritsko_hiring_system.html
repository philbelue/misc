Why This Exists
Problem Statement

Packaging and fulfillment is the primary throughput bottleneck — not production. Every week without a dedicated hire is a week Eric stays buried in operations instead of generating revenue. This system installs the hiring, screening, and onboarding process so Leighanna can execute independently and Eric can stay focused on sales.

What it solves

Removes fulfillment ops from Eric's plate. Creates a repeatable process for finding and onboarding reliable floor workers.

What it enables

Leighanna runs hiring and management independently. Standards exist before the first hire walks in the door.

How it scales

Each hire adds capacity without chaos. The system grows as the team grows — same process, more people.

In-Person Interview
6 Questions — Ask Every One, In Order

Left = strong answer. Right = red flag. Use the notes field on the scorecard tab to capture specifics as you go.

Q1Walk me through a typical day at your last job.
✓ Strong Answer

Specific. Sequential. Describes what "done" looks like. Shows comfort with structured repetitive work without complaining about it.

✕ Red Flag

Vague. Jumps around. Complains about management unprompted. Can't describe a normal day in concrete terms.

Notes
Q2Tell me about a time you caught a mistake before it became a problem.
✓ Strong Answer

Specific example. Took ownership. Fixed it or flagged it immediately. Doesn't need follow-up prompting to get the details.

✕ Red Flag

Can't think of one. Blames someone else. Describes a case where they let it go or hid it from their manager.

Notes
Q3How do you handle it when the pace picks up and things get busy?
✓ Strong Answer

Describes a method or personal system. Stays calm in the description. Explicitly says they don't skip steps under pressure.

✕ Red Flag

"I just work harder." No specifics. Or visibly flustered just describing a busy scenario — that's a preview of on-floor behavior.

Notes
Q4If you realized mid-shift you'd been doing something the wrong way, what would you do?
✓ Strong Answer

Stops immediately. Flags it. Asks for correction. Doesn't try to self-fix silently or finish the task first and mention it later.

✕ Red Flag

Finishes the task anyway. Hides it. "I'd just keep going and mention it later." These are the errors that compound at volume.

Notes
Q5What's the longest you've held a job, and why did you leave?
✓ Strong Answer

1+ year. Left voluntarily for a growth, pay, or personal reason. Talks about it plainly without over-explaining or hedging.

✕ Red Flag

Fired. Multiple short stints. Vague or evasive about the reason. Gets defensive when you ask a natural follow-up question.

Notes
Q6Is there anything that would prevent you from being here reliably Monday through [day]?
✓ Strong Answer

Clear and direct. No hesitation. Confirms availability without conditions or "it depends on…" qualifiers.

✕ Red Flag

Hesitation. "It depends." Conditions or caveats. A vague "I'll figure it out" — reliability issues surface here first.

Wrap-Up — Ask Every Time
"Do you have any questions for me about the role or how we work?"

Curious candidates ask something. What they ask reveals what they're actually thinking about.

"What would make this a great fit for you?"

Reveals alignment. Red flag if they list things you can't deliver. Green flag if they describe what the role actually offers.

"When can you start?"

Direct availability test. Hesitation here after they already confirmed availability on the phone = inconsistency worth noting.

Make the hire decision the same day. Tell them when they'll hear back — give a specific day, not "we'll be in touch." Delaying doesn't improve the decision. It costs you good candidates to faster employers.

Day 1 — Orient & Observe
Goal: They understand the full flow before touching anything alone
Tour the facility — production, packaging, staging, suppliesShow where everything lives and explain the sequence before any tasks begin.
Walk the full order → pack → label → ship flow with themDo it physically with a real order. Don't just describe it.
Work beside them for the first 2–3 hours on live tasksThis is calibration, not hand-holding. You're setting the standard in real time.
Show them one completed "good" packed order — explicitlySay it out loud: "This is what a correct order looks like." Make the standard tangible.
Cover facility basics — safety, equipment, supplies, escalationEmergency exits, what they'll operate, where supplies are, who to ask if you're unavailable.
End-of-day conversation: "What was confusing?"What they ask tells you more than observing them. A good hire asks specific questions.
Days 2–3 — Independent With Check-Ins
Goal: They work alone. You verify quality is holding.
Assign task set before the shift starts — quantity, task, deadlineWritten is better than verbal. Ambiguity about the target creates drift.
Check in at 60–90 minute intervals — not constantlySet checkpoints. Don't hover. Hovering signals distrust and creates dependence.
Do not check in more frequently than every 60 minutes
Spot-check 10–15% of completed work — unannouncedCheck: label accuracy, quantity, packaging condition. Random, not announced.
Correct any errors same day, in personShow the right way together. Don't just note it — that's too late at this stage.
Same-day correction is non-negotiable in Week 1
Note: are they asking questions or guessing silently?Asking = green flag. Silent guessing = address immediately. This pattern sets in fast.
Days 4–5 — Full Load + Measure
Goal: Establish baseline throughput and quality rate
Assign full day's volume with a specific targete.g., "40 orders packed and staged by 2pm." Measurable and time-bound.
Track actual vs. target — informallyDon't penalize shortfall on Day 4. Use it to calibrate real capacity and identify setup issues.
Spot-check quality — same rigor as Days 2–3Don't reduce quality checks because pace is increasing. Quality monitoring is permanent.
Identify what's slowing them down — process or person?Unclear setup, missing tools, or workspace layout issues are fixable. Attitude isn't.
End of Week 1 — Review Conversation
10 minutes. Informal. Covers both directions.
Ask: "How's it going? What's still unclear?"Start with their experience before sharing yours. You'll get more useful information.
Share what you've noticed — one thing well, one to improveSpecific and direct. No fluff. Name the behavior, not a judgment about the person.
Confirm: do they understand the quality standard?If not, reset it clearly now. Don't let ambiguity carry into Week 2.

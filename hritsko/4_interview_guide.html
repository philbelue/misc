Before You Meet
Phone Screen Checklist
Do this before any in-person interview. If any of these fail, don't schedule a meeting. You're screening for real availability and basic fit — not character.
  • Fewer than 2 jobs in the last 12 months — or gaps are explainedAsk directly: "Can you walk me through your last year or two of work?" Listen for honesty and continuity.
  • Can start within 1 weekFor Hire 1, timing matters. Ask: "If this is a fit, how soon could you start?"
  • Availability confirmed for fixed shift — no conditionsState the exact schedule. Ask if there's anything that would prevent them from being there consistently.
  • Transportation is specific and reliableAsk: "How would you be getting here each day?" Vague answers = a real risk.
The phone screen takes 5–7 minutes. You're not selling the role yet — you're filtering. If all four pass, schedule the in-person and move to the Questions tab.
Structure
How the Interview Should Flow
01
Welcome + set context (2 min)
Briefly describe the role and what a typical day looks like. Don't oversell. Be direct about pace and repetition — you want someone who wants that.
02
Run the 6 questions (15–18 min)
Ask every question in order. Don't skip. Take notes on the scorecard while they answer — details fade after the conversation.
Do not skip questions to speed up the interview
03
Wrap-up questions + close (5 min)
Give them space to ask questions. Tell them when they'll hear back — and follow through on that timeline.
04
Score within 30 minutes
Complete the scorecard immediately after. Make the hire/pass/second-interview call the same day.

In-Person Interview
6 Questions — Ask Every One, In Order

Left column = what a strong answer looks like. Right column = what to watch for. Take notes on the scorecard as you go.

Q1Walk me through a typical day at your last job.
✓ Strong Answer

Specific. Sequential. Describes what "done" looks like. Shows comfort with structured, repetitive work without complaining about it.

✕ Red Flag

Vague. Jumps around. Complains about management or coworkers unprompted. Can't describe a normal day in concrete terms.

Notes
Q2Tell me about a time you caught a mistake before it became a problem.
✓ Strong Answer

Specific example. Took ownership. Fixed it or flagged it immediately. Doesn't require follow-up prompting to get details.

✕ Red Flag

Can't think of one. Blames someone else for the situation. Describes a case where they let it go or hid it.

Notes
Q3How do you handle it when the pace picks up and things get busy?
✓ Strong Answer

Describes a method or personal system. Stays calm in the description. Says they don't skip steps to go faster.

✕ Red Flag

"I just work harder." No specifics. Or visibly flustered just describing a busy scenario — that's a preview of on-floor behavior.

Notes
Q4If you realized mid-shift you'd been doing something the wrong way, what would you do?
✓ Strong Answer

Stops immediately. Flags it. Asks for correction. Doesn't try to self-fix silently or finish out the task first.

✕ Red Flag

Finishes the task anyway. Hides it. "I'd just keep going and mention it later." These are the errors that compound.

Notes
Q5What's the longest you've held a job, and why did you leave?
✓ Strong Answer

1+ year. Left voluntarily for a growth, pay, or personal reason. Talks about it plainly without over-explaining or hedging.

✕ Red Flag

Fired. Multiple short stints. Vague or evasive about the reason. Gets defensive when you ask a natural follow-up.

Notes
Q6Is there anything that would prevent you from being here reliably Monday through [day]?
✓ Strong Answer

Clear and direct. No hesitation. Confirms availability without conditions or "it depends on…" qualifiers.

✕ Red Flag

Hesitation. "It depends." Conditions, caveats, or a vague "I'll figure it out." Reliability issues surface here first.

Notes
Close the Interview
Wrap-Up Questions — Ask Every Time

These three questions close the interview and reveal things the structured questions won't. Don't skip them.

"Do you have any questions for me about the role or how we work?"

Curious candidates ask something. Indifferent ones don't. What they ask tells you what they're actually thinking about.

Their Questions / Notes
"What would make this a great fit for you?"

Reveals alignment. Red flag if they can't answer or list things you clearly can't deliver. Green flag if they describe what the role actually offers.

Notes
"When can you start?"

A direct availability test. Hesitation here after they already confirmed availability on the phone = inconsistency worth noting.

After the Interview
Closing Checklist
  • Tell them when they'll hear back — and give a specific timeframeVague "we'll be in touch" erodes candidate trust. Say a day: "You'll hear from me by [day]."
  • Complete the scorecard within 30 minutes of the interview endingDetails fade fast. Score while it's fresh.
  • File the completed scorecard in /Candidates/ folderOne file per candidate. Name it: YYYY-MM-DD_[LastName].pdf or .html
  • Follow through on the timeline you gave themEven a "not moving forward" response keeps your reputation intact for future hiring.
The hiring decision should be made the same day. Delaying doesn't improve the decision — it just creates uncertainty and costs you good candidates to faster employers.

Day 1 — Orient & Observe
Goal: They understand the full flow before touching anything alone
Tour the facilityProduction → packaging area → shipping staging → supplies. Show where everything lives. Explain the sequence.
Walk the full order → pack → label → ship flowDo it with them. Don't just describe it — physically walk through a real order.
Work beside them for first 2–3 hours on live tasksThis is calibration, not hand-holding. You're setting the standard in real time.
Show them one completed "good" packed orderThis is the quality reference. Say it explicitly: "This is what a correct order looks like."
Cover safety and facility basicsEmergency exits, equipment they'll touch, where to find supplies, who to ask if you're not available.
End-of-day conversation: "What was confusing?"What they ask tells you more than observing them. A good hire asks specific questions.
Days 2–3 — Independent With Check-Ins
Goal: They work alone. You verify quality is holding.
Assign full task set before shift startsQuantity target, task, and deadline stated clearly before they begin. Written is better than verbal.
Check in at 60–90 minute intervals — not constantlySet checkpoints. Don't hover. Hovering signals distrust and creates dependence.
Spot-check 10–15% of completed work — unannouncedCheck for: label accuracy, quantity, packaging condition. Random, not announced.
Correct any errors same day, in personShow the right way together. Don't just note it for later — later is too late at this stage.
Watch for: asks questions vs. guessesAsking = green flag. Silent guessing = address immediately. This pattern sets in fast.
Days 4–5 — Full Load + Measure
Goal: Establish baseline throughput and quality rate
Assign full day's volume with a targetExample: "40 orders packed and staged by 2pm." Make it specific and measurable.
Track actual vs. target — informallyDon't penalize shortfall on Day 4. Use it to calibrate real capacity and identify bottlenecks in the setup, not the person.
Spot-check quality — same rigor as Days 2–3Don't reduce quality checks just because pace is increasing. Quality monitoring is permanent.
Note patterns: what's slowing them down?Process confusion? Physical setup? Missing tools? Identify the cause before labeling it a performance issue.
End of Week 1 — Review Conversation
10 minutes. Informal. Covers both directions.
Ask: "How's it going? What's still unclear?"Start with their experience before sharing yours. You'll get more useful information.
Share what you've noticed — specific and directName one thing they're doing well. Name one thing to improve. No fluff, no sandwiching.
Confirm: do they understand the quality standard?If not, reset it clearly now. Don't let ambiguity about standards carry into Week 2.
Make your keep/act judgment callSee the End-of-Week Verdict tab. Make the call before the week 2 schedule is set.
Week 1 Assessment
Keep or Act?

Make this call at the end of Week 1 — before the week 2 schedule is set. Waiting doesn't improve the decision.

✓ Keep — Green Flags
Shows up on time every day without follow-up
Knows the workflow without being prompted by Day 5
Error rate is low — even if pace is still building
Asks questions before guessing
Takes correction without becoming defensive
✕ Act Now — Red Flags
Late or absent more than once in Week 1
Guesses instead of asking — repeatedly, after correction
Quality errors persist after being shown the right way
Attitude issues or defensiveness to direct feedback
Starts strong, visibly disengages by Day 3–4
If you're seeing red flags by Day 3, don't wait until Friday. Address it directly the same day. Correction at Day 3 is easy. Correction at Week 3 is a habit fight.
Trust your gut — but make sure it's not about pace. A slow but accurate, reliable hire in Week 1 is a better outcome than a fast, sloppy one. Pace builds. Reliability is fixed.

Ongoing Management Rhythm

Once someone is past the ramp period, these become the recurring structure. Consistency is the system.

Daily
Before shift: state task, quantity, deadline
Midday checkpoint — on track?
Random quality spot-check (10–15% of output)
End of shift: any blockers to note?
Weekly
One short feedback conversation (5–10 min)
What's working → name it specifically
One thing to improve → name it specifically
Any process changes to communicate
Consistency is the management system. Same standards, same check-in rhythm, every day. Inconsistency creates confusion about what's expected — and that's where quality problems start.
Management Principles
How to Handle Common Situations
SituationLikely CauseResponse
Moving too slowSlow + confusedTraining problem. Re-show the process. Be specific about what's unclear.
Moving too slowSlow + knows the processPerformance conversation. Set a target. Give a timeline. Be direct.
Sloppy workRushing or unclear standardStop the work together. Correct the error. Reset the standard explicitly.
Attendance issuesOne-off vs. patternOne instance: note it. Three instances: decision point. Document immediately.
Defensive to feedbackFeedback style or actual resistanceSeparate observation from judgment: "I noticed X" not "you always do Y."
Over-reliance on youDidn't build independence in rampStep back intentionally. Answer with questions: "What do you think you should do?"
Your job as the leader: make it easy for them to do good work. Clear instructions. Clear space. Clear standards. Remove obstacles. That's it.
